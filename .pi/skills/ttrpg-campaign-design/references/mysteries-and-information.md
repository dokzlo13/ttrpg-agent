# Mysteries and information

Design information so players can make meaningful decisions without guessing the
GM's intended chain of logic.

## Hidden chronology versus discovery

For a complex mystery, first establish the hidden causal chronology: what
actually happened, who acted, what evidence it left, and what changed afterward.
This chronology is GM truth, not a required discovery order.

Then design revelations that can emerge through several situations or methods.
Do not turn chronology into a sequence the players must reconstruct one step at a
time.

## Revelations

A revelation is a conclusion that enables understanding, access, or a meaningful
decision. It is not an individual clue.

```markdown
### Revelation: <conclusion>

- **Why it matters:** decision or access it enables.
- **Routes:** independent clues, witnesses, actions, or methods.
- **Knowledge asymmetry:** who knows, who is mistaken, and what truthful evidence
  or action can expose the difference.
- **Recovery:** causal way new information can enter play if absence would stall
  or unfairly distort the situation.
```

No necessary conclusion should depend on one fragile source. Use two or more
independent routes when practical; prefer three for a conclusion whose absence
would block major play. Do not invent filler clues to satisfy a count.

## Clue quality

A useful clue is:

- available without an unnecessary single-roll lockout;
- concrete enough to notice and discuss;
- actionable—it changes understanding or points somewhere;
- consistent with the hidden truth;
- movable when another reasonable approach could reveal the same fact.

Checks can determine speed, detail, cost, danger, or additional advantage without
making the only essential clue disappear.

Prepared clues are a safety net, not a closed list. A sound unplanned
investigation should derive truthful information from the established situation.

## Information fairness

For a consequential choice, ask:

- What can the party reasonably know before choosing?
- What remains uncertain by design?
- Which actor knows or believes each relevant claim?
- Is an apparent contradiction bias, ignorance, deception, or an actual design
  error?
- Can players test important claims through action?

NPCs and factions know only what their position, communication, and experience
justify. Do not give every actor the GM's explanation.

When information material to an active force's next move is learned, ask whether
a willing messenger, usable channel, incentive, and enough time exist for it to
travel. Propagate, distort, delay, or contain it according to the fiction; group
membership alone does not grant shared knowledge. Do not build propagation on a
disputed messenger, channel, or baseline knowledge state: preserve the
higher-authority state and present any alternate transmission only as an explicit
conditional proposal. Record only a material change in knowledge or action in
the natural owning note when useful—never a new register.

Prefer genuine bias, partial testimony, and mistaken belief over artificial red
herrings. A deliberate false lead should reveal conflict or character and have a
fair route to refutation.

## Proactive information

If play stalls, an actor or changing situation can create a new opportunity:
approach, bargain, threat, mistake, movement of evidence, or visible escalation.
It should reveal information and preserve choice, not deliver the intended
solution by force.

## Promises and foreshadowing

A player-visible setup creates an expectation even when its exact payoff remains
open. In the campaign note that already owns the thread, record only what helps:

- status: `open`, `advanced`, `paid off`, or `deliberately retired`;
- what players have actually seen or learned;
- possible function of a future payoff, not a fixed ending;
- constraints the payoff must respect;
- latest useful point to decide.

Do not create one file per promise or require every distant setup to resolve.
Retirement can be deliberate when play has made a thread irrelevant.
