# Factions, pressure, and consequences

Autonomous forces make the campaign world move. Pressure should arise from goals,
capability, and visible fictional triggers—not from the GM needing the next plot
beat.

## Active force essentials

Fully detail only a force material to active or reachable play:

```markdown
### <Force>

- **Want:** current objective and why it matters now.
- **Knowledge limit:** what it knows, hides, or believes incorrectly.
- **Tools and constraints:** usable leverage plus meaningful limits.
- **Present action:** what it is doing now.
- **Next visible move:** what it does if unopposed and how that becomes legible.
```

Add reaction principles only for a force likely to improvise repeatedly against
or alongside the party. For distant forces, record function and constraints;
leave unknown details open.

## Fair pressure

Before an irreversible escalation, check:

1. The threat or opportunity was observable.
2. Players had a meaningful chance to respond or knowingly chose elsewhere.
3. The trigger follows from fiction, not elapsed sessions.
4. The consequence creates a new playable state rather than deleting play.

Normal investigation within the crisis is not delay unless the tradeoff is clear.

## Ladders and clocks

Use a pressure ladder or clock only when the campaign already tracks one, the
user asks for it, or a gradual fictional change is otherwise difficult to hold in
working memory.

A tracker records the world; it does not cause events. Define the condition,
fictional triggers, visible signs, and concrete full-state consequence. Do not
tick it because “another session passed.”

## Reactions, not branches

Useful reaction principles describe priorities, for example:

- bargain before spending scarce force;
- protect reputation over assets;
- preserve the objective while changing method;
- retreat when cost exceeds the goal;
- sacrifice a pawn to hide the operation.

Do not enumerate reactions to imaginary player plans.

## Consequence design

Prepare only the two or three materially different world states the present
situation makes useful. Depending on the conflict, these may come from success,
compromise, partial resolution, failure, refusal, alliance, exposure, or betrayal.

A materially different state changes one or more of:

- relationships or faction position;
- information and public belief;
- resources, access, or obligations;
- danger and visible pressure;
- future opportunities.

For an invested NPC or faction materially affected by a prepared state, note the
observable change or unresolved pressure relevant to play. Do not force a final
fate, resolve off-screen uncertainty, or account for every stakeholder.

Failure should create cost, pressure, changed access, or a new problem—not a dead
end. Important content should survive a failed roll unless irreversible risk was
legible and meaningful.

When a delayed consequence would otherwise feel arbitrary or unrelated, prepare
at least one discoverable trace to its cause, proportionate to what the fiction
would reveal. Do not force immediate attribution, expose a hidden actor
prematurely, or require an NPC to explain the GM's causal model.

Convergence can preserve agency when approaches change costs, relationships,
information, timing, access, or other stakes, or when a shared outcome follows
from an established causal constraint. Flag collapse only when materially
different feasible choices repeatedly erase the consequential differences the
players could reasonably affect. Inspect the full surrounding design before
calling it railroading.

## Compatibility at intersections

Only when two designed threads can reconverge, check that their resulting NPC
status, faction position, public belief, resources, and access can coexist. Fix a
real contradiction, but do not simulate every theoretical path or introduce a
variable registry.
