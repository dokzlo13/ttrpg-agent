# Situation design

A situation is a reusable piece of present-tense world state. It can be a
location, NPC, faction operation, unstable problem, event, resource, or
revelation hub. It is not a predicted scene or a branch in a scripted story.

## Prep depth

Use only the detail appropriate to reachability:

- **Active:** enough to run and react now.
- **Reachable:** causal role, actors, stakes, ways in, and useful connections.
- **Distant:** function, constraints, promises, and open questions only.

Do not detail distant material merely because it exists.

## Situation essentials

For an important situation, record only what is useful:

```markdown
### <Situation>

- **Instability and stakes:** what can change here.
- **Active forces:** who wants what here.
- **Ways in:** how it becomes reachable or relevant.
- **Leads/resources:** information, people, access, or leverage available here.
- **State changes:** what changes if engaged, transformed, bypassed, or ignored.
```

Add defenses, sensory details, encounter elements, or reaction guidance only when
the task needs them. Do not complete fields for appearance's sake.

## Reachability

Important content should not depend on one brittle prerequisite. Use multiple
ways in when the fiction supports them:

- another NPC or faction;
- a clue from a different situation;
- environmental evidence;
- a consequence of another thread;
- direct player initiative;
- a causal proactive event.

A hard dependency is justified only when it is genuinely causal. Ask whether it
could instead be a cost, risk, bargaining problem, or alternate route.

## Tools, not contingencies

Prepare what actors can use:

- personnel and relationships;
- locations and routes;
- information and secrets;
- authority, money, favors, equipment, rituals, defenses, and liabilities.

Then improvise from motives. Do not enumerate every possible player action.

## Connections

Connect situations when one naturally:

- reveals another;
- controls or changes access;
- supplies leverage or a resource;
- threatens or competes with another force;
- changes another situation's state.

Label the relation only when its causal meaning is unclear. One useful connection
is better than forced links to every campaign thread.

## Likely orientation versus required order

A likely near-term route can help the GM orient, especially when content is
already prepared. Label it as likely and preserve alternate routes, interruptions,
and returns. Never convert “this is nearby” or “this is prepared in Foundry” into
“the party must do this next.”

When auditing an existing graph, inspect every inbound route and the surrounding
prose before calling a dependency mandatory. A narrow or outdated diagram can
bias table use without proving that the underlying design is a railroad; report
presentation bias separately from an actual content lock.
